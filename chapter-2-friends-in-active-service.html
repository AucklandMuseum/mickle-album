Saying goodbye to sons and daughters

Alice Mickle described her souvenir photo album as a collection of photographs of friends who left New Zealand and who volunteered their services to fight in the Great War.

All over the country communities were gathering to farewell troops as they left for the front. Many women signed up to serve as nurses.

As the wife of the local doctor, Alice was acquainted with many Birkenhead families who were saying goodbye to their sons and daughters. She kept in touch with them as they served in Europe and the Middle East.​

Soldier portraits

Throughout the war Alice sent parcels to soldiers – the sons, fathers, husbands and brothers from families that she and Dr Mickle knew personally. As long as she had the correct regimental numbers, her parcels reached their destinations.

The soldiers thanked Alice by sending photographs – usually formal studio portraits commissioned as a keepsake for families and friends left at home. Some photographs were taken while on leave from the fighting in Europe or the Middle East.

Alice collected these photos in her album, captioning each one with details about the individual’s service.

 

Birkenhead’s contribution to the war

At the turn of the century, the population of Birkenhead was just over a thousand. Between 1914 and 1918, 261 Birkenhead residents left to serve overseas. 31 men did not come home.

Many returned traumatised and wounded.

Memorial cards

Newspapers published official lists of casualties during the war; a family’s loss was known to their wider community.

The studio portraits of soldiers who did not return from war became objects for grieving and remembrance.

Families attempted to practice bereavement rituals, despite their separation from the graves of their loved ones. Those who could afford it sent memorial cards in response to letters and telegrams of condolence.
