Alice Mickle: Friend of the soldiers

At the outbreak of the First World War, Birkenhead resident Alice Mickle began sending gifts to her friends in active service.

Mrs Alice Mickle

Mrs Alice Mickle and her husband moved to Birkenhead in 1912 (or 1911?). Dr Arthur Mickle worked as a doctor in the borough. He also had a consulting room and surgery in the nearby suburb of Northcote. As the wife of the local doctor, Alice knew many of the young men who left for war.

Like many other New Zealand women, Alice contributed to the war effort by sending gifts to soldiers. “Practically as soon as the war broke out,” wrote a journalist for the Auckland Star, “Mrs Mickle began to make up parcels to send away, and as time went on and she knew exactly where to send them and precisely what was most needed, her activities doubled and trebled.”

 

Birkenhead’s close-knit communities

The communities of Birkenhead were exceptionally close-knit. This was due, in part, to the borough’s remove. Roads were notoriously rough and residents relied upon ferries to transport them to Auckland city.

Geographic isolation bound families together. In medical emergencies families depended on help from their neighbours and the expertise of Alice’s husband, Dr Mickle.

 

The role of the local doctor

As the local doctor, Dr Mickle was intimately involved in the lives of Birkenhead residents.

He visited their homes - helping to deliver babies, attending to the sick or dying, and comforting the bereaved.

Through Arthur and no doubt on her own accord, Alice formed close connections with Birkenhead families.
